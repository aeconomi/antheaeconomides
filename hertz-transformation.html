Hertz Digital Transformation

Project Summary

In order to keep up with technology, expectations, and competition in the marketplace, the Hertz.com Digital Transformation project aimed to unify the disconnected legacy platforms and redesign the Hertz digital experience. The project used an agile release process to deploy different sections of the website in order to launch more quickly and facilitate customer feedback/customer-focused development. As part of the content team, my role was to help migrate the 2,000+ pages (not including translations) onto an up-to-date CMS platform, ensuring the content was brand approved and SEO optimized. This was a huge project and required input from many different departments, but I personally collaborated with the IBM development, UX, and Marketing teams on a day-to-day basis.

Team Members & Collaborators

IBM Garage, UX, Loyalty, Analytics team, and Marketing Stakeholders

Tools

Photoshop, Adobe XD, Adobe Experience Manager, Excel

Plan

To stay on top of tight timelines, we loaded “base” content (content that we took from the legacy site that could be used as a starting point for the new site) into the new CMS platform. We prioritized this content according to the release schedule and loaded the booking process first since the reservation flow was the first section to be released.


It was also important our team worked side-by-side with the UX and design teams to establish content as a foundational starting point for the planning, design, and development phases and to make sure the end solution met business requirements. We regularly reported up to the Product Owners to make sure we were on track and targets were met for each sprint. We worked in weekly iterations with the PO’s regularly re-prioritizing the backlog to focus on impactful project work.

Strategy & Implementation

The website was being migrated to a new version of AEM, which allowed for increased scalability and control over the content. The new CMS also had page templates that were a part of the design refresh and allowed for more consistency across the site. While the first phase was focused on migrating the Hertz website, the plan was to reutilize the code base and develop a list of features called a common core library that could be used for the Dollar and Thrifty website redesigns as well. This meant a lot of the generic content used on rental car websites could be reused for all three brands, giving us a leg up on the other transformations and reducing development costs.


We planned to roll out the website to different regions and dialects in an iterative fashion as well. With hertz.com serving customers in over 150 countries, each source market would have content provided in English (US English or UK English) and many would have their region’s local dialect as well. In total, we had to plan and prep content to support 32 different dialects.

Iterate

Working in iterative development cycles, we used A/B testing and user feedback to make content updates and inform development as sections were released. Design blockers to conversions were identified, enabling us to fix them and watch conversion analytics to confirm issues were resolving. In sessions using Clicktale, we found that one of the biggest pain-points for customers was site structure and search functionality. We ended up shortening each menu title for simplicity and added search, chat, and contact information in the header to make it easier for customers to find what they were looking for.

Summary

The new reservation flow rolled out to US and Canadian customers in early March, right around when covid-19 shutdowns began. The virus took a direct hit on the travel industry so it was hard to measure the impact the redesign had on conversions and eCommerce revenue. However, we were able to measure the impact on page load speed right away. Page load times dropped from 6 seconds to 3-4 seconds- almost a 50% decrease! Code refactoring played a role in this but also reducing file sizes and compressing images.


I learned so much from working on this project and all the logistics that go into a website migration and redesign. It was interesting to be a part of each stage- to develop and launch new features, conduct testing, then make changes based on our findings. Most often, the small differences had the biggest impact. Removing unnecessary steps and popups from the checkout process was a primary driver in increased conversion rates.


One big learning was that creating consistent page templates and a single design system is vital for a unified brand experience but it's also important to build templates and components that allow for as much flexibility as possible as priorities and marketing initiatives change over time.


The project had many challenges, but one big difficulty stemmed from the sheer amount of people involved. People were constantly rolling on and off the project so it was tough keeping everyone on the same page. We also ended up changing vendors midway, so knowledge transfer sessions and documentation throughout the process was extremely important. Another difficulty was communicating with remote teams in different time zones. Slack made back-and-forth updates more fluid but I also found recording meetings and training sessions helpful. Our daily standups also kept team members aligned during each stage of the project and ensured there was no overlap in responsibilities.
