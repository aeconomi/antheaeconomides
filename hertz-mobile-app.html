Hertz Mobile App

Project Summary

The old Hertz app was outdated, crashed frequently, and required development in order to update content. In partnering with IBM, our team worked with the Marketing, UX, and Creative Services teams to deliver the new Hertz Mobile App. The new Hertz app gives customers a faster rental experience, allowing users to quickly login with touch ID, manage their rewards, and find nearby locations in an updated user interface. My role was to help provide and maintain the content, and design templates that could be used to collaborate with brand and development teams. I also played a role in marketing the launch of the app by building landing pages and posting banners and videos on the website to help drive awareness.

Team Members & Collaborators

IBM Garage, UX, Loyalty, Analytics team, and Marketing Stakeholders

Tools

Photoshop, Adobe XD, Adobe Experience Manager, Excel

Analysis

One of the biggest problems customers had with the old app was that it either froze, crashed, or was slow to load. Because of this, giving our customers better speed and convenience during their rental journey was a primary focus for the new app.


Some of the top uses of the old Hertz app was to:


1. Book a Reservation
2. Modify/Cancel Reservations
3. Checking Loyalty Information & Point Balance
4. Update Account Information
5. Find Return Location Directions


Since the previous app redirected customers to the website for three of the five top uses, it was important that we included all of these uses in the new mobile app. We also wanted the app to help answer some of the top questions asked by customers:


  • Where’s my reservation number?
  • How many points do I have?
  • What is my Gold Plus Rewards account number?
  • Looking to find receipts?
  • Need to update credit card?
  • Find parking services near me?
  • What is returning address of location?
  • Looking to change arrival time?
  • Looking to bypass vehicle counter?



The plan was to launch with these core features but to continue to iterate to deliver new functionality that would help us surpass our competitors.

As part of the content strategy we needed to appeal to these 3 different segments but also reduce unnecessary content and keep things simple and easy to digest. Including a fleet guide with high-res vehicle images would appeal to Car Enthusiasts. Promotions featuring vacation getaways and special offers would attract Deal Seekers. Travel tips and Gold Plus Rewards benefits would appeal to Quality Confidants in addition to offering essential information about their rental car journey.

Plan

Since the previous app had been very limiting in terms of maintenance and updates- all updates had to be done by developers- it was our goal to have the CMS host as much content as possible, enabling our team to swap out imagery, text, or CTA’s as marketing initiatives and priorities changed.

Design

The design and layout was built by the IBM development team along with our in-house UX and design teams but it was my job to create imagery templates that could be used to ensure there was a consistent visual appearance and brand reinforcement throughout the app.


It was important that the imagery and graphics displayed in the app shared similar characteristics with the website and aligned with the new Brand Guidelines. Hertz rebranded in 2017 and the new look and feel was black and white with a pop of yellow to emphasize the Hertz yellow.

While this color scheme was eye-catching and a great opportunity for brand reinforcement, it made it tough to find imagery that had high enough contrast to pass web accessibility standards when overlaid with text. We had to be very careful we were adhering to color-blind standards. We also needed to follow the new brand guidelines which had changed from using dark photography with heavy black accents to incorporating light and airy images with purposeful pops of yellow.


Overall we needed to solve two problems in the imagery designs:


1. Make the Text Readable
2. Minimize Dark Imagery


We came up with 3 different image treatments solutions to present to brand.


1. Overlay entire image
2. Solid color block behind text
3. Scrim Overlay


In the end, the scrim overlay was chosen because it was a subtle enough design change that didn’t affect the integrity of the image while also improving text readability.


For photography, three categories reflected the Hertz visual identity: Vehicle, Business and Leisure. For the image selections, multiple options were presented to the Brand team (iterations shown below). The selections were chosen based on those that communicated an elevated and effortless travel experience, while also incorporating large amounts of yellow- the biggest opportunity for branding.

Strategy & Implementation

The website was being migrated to page templates on a new version of AEM, which allowed for increased scalability and contsistency throughout the site. While the first phase was focused on migrating the Hertz website, the plan was to reutilize the code base and develop a list of features called a common core library that could be used for the Dollar and Thrifty website redesigns as well. This meant a lot of the generic content could be reused for all three brands, giving us a leg up on the transformations for the other Brands while reducing development costs.


We planned to roll out the website to different regions and dialects in an iterative fashion. With hertz.com serving customers in over 150 countries, each source market would have content provided in English (US English or UK English) and many would have their region’s local dialect as well. In total, we had to plan and prep content to support 32 different dialects.

Iterate

Working in iterative development cycles, we used A/B testing and user feedback to make content updates and inform development as sections were released. If design blockers to conversions were identified, we had processes in place that allowed us to pivot quickly to fix issues. In sessions using Clicktale, our analytics team found that one of the biggest pain-points for customers was site structure and search functionality. We ended up shortening each menu title for simplicity and added search, chat, and contact information in the header to make it easier for customers to find what they were looking for.

A/B testing helped design our new navigation, which simplified our information architecture and led more customers into the booking funnel.

Summary

The new reservation flow rolled out to US and Canadian customers in early March, right around when covid-19 shutdowns began. The virus took a direct hit on the travel industry so it was hard to measure the impact the redesign had on conversions and eCommerce revenue. However, we were able to measure the impact on page load speed right away. Page load times dropped from 6 seconds to 3-4 seconds- almost a 50% decrease! Code refactoring played a role in this but also reducing file sizes and compressing images.


I learned so much from working on this project and all the logistics that go into a website migration and redesign. It was interesting to be a part of each stage- to develop and launch new features, conduct testing, then make changes based on our findings. Most often, the small differences had the biggest impact. Removing unnecessary steps and popups from the checkout process was a primary driver in increased conversion rates.


One big learning was that creating consistent page templates and a single design system is vital for a unified brand experience but it's also important to build templates and components that allow for as much flexibility as possible as priorities and marketing initiatives change over time.


The project had many challenges, but one big difficulty stemmed from the sheer amount of people involved. People were constantly rolling on and off the project so it was tough keeping everyone on the same page. Another difficulty was communicating with remote teams in different time zones. Slack made back-and-forth updates more fluid but I also found recording meetings and training sessions helpful. Our daily standups also kept team members aligned during each stage of the project and ensured there was no overlap in responsibilities.
